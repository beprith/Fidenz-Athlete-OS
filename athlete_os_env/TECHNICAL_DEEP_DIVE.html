01System Architecture Overview

Fidenz Athlete OS is a complete reinforcement learning environment that simulates professional athletes as AI personas. An external agent interacts via the standard OpenEnv reset() / step(action) / state API, receiving observations and shaped rewards.

The data flows through six specialized agents coordinated by a central Orchestrator using a shared blackboard pattern. The RL engine (PPO + KL constraint) shapes the reward signal and gently adjusts persona traits across simulation steps.

0216-Dimensional Persona Model

Each athlete is represented as a behavioral persona encoded in a 16-dimensional trait vector. This compact representation captures the full behavioral fingerprint of a player.

The trait vector $\mathbf{t} \in \mathbb{R}^{16}$ is normalized to the probability simplex for policy operations:

$$\pi(\mathbf{t})_i = \frac{e^{t_i}}{\sum_{j=1}^{16} e^{t_j}} \quad \text{(softmax normalization)}$$

This ensures trait weights sum to 1 and can be interpreted as a probability distribution over behavioral dimensions.

03Reinforcement Learning Engine

The RL engine operates on a lightweight, non-neural architecture designed to run within the 2vCPU / 8GB memory constraint of Hugging Face Spaces. Instead of training a deep network, it directly optimizes the 16-dimensional persona weight vector.

Key Design Principle

Traditional RL uses neural networks to learn a policy $\pi_\theta(a|s)$. Our approach replaces the network with a logit vector $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{16}$ whose softmax gives trait weights. This is mathematically equivalent to a single-layer softmax policy over a fixed-dimensional action space.

Each step produces a shaped reward $r_t$, which is then penalized by the KL-divergence from a frozen baseline persona. The PPO policy adjusts trait logits every 5 steps using a clipped surrogate objective. The experience replay buffer stores transitions for off-policy advantage estimation.

04Reward Shaping & Bounded Sigmoid

The reward signal provides partial credit per step, not just a sparse terminal score. It combines four weighted components passed through a bounded sigmoid.

4.1 Weighted Component Score

$$R_{\text{raw}} = 0.40 \cdot S_{\text{output}} + 0.30 \cdot S_{\text{tactical}} + 0.20 \cdot S_{\text{coherence}} + 0.10 \cdot S_{\text{efficiency}}$$
ComponentWeightSource
Output Score40%Goals, assists, key contributions normalized by rounds played
Tactical Fit30%Player rating divided by 10.0 (capped at 1.0)
Coherence Score20%Diversity of event types; penalizes monotonic outputs
Step Efficiency10%Inversely proportional to wasted simulation rounds

4.2 Penalty Terms

Two penalties are applied before the sigmoid to penalize undesirable agent behavior:

  • Hallucination Penalty: $P_h = -0.3$ when implausible stats are detected
  • Repeat Penalty: $P_r = -0.2$ when the same action type is repeated 3+ consecutive times

4.3 Bounded Sigmoid

$$R_{\text{shaped}} = \sigma\!\left(R_{\text{raw}} + P_h + P_r\right) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-k(x - m)}}$$

with steepness $k = 6.0$ and midpoint $m = 0.5$. The output is clipped to $[0, 1]$.

The sigmoid ensures rewards are bounded and provides a smooth gradient around the midpoint. Small changes in performance near the middle range produce meaningful reward differences, while extreme scores are compressed.

4.4 Final Shaped Reward

$$r_t = \max\!\left(0,\; R_{\text{shaped}} - \beta \cdot D_{\text{KL}}\!\left(\pi_t \| \pi_0\right)\right)$$

The KL penalty (Section 5) is subtracted from the shaped reward to prevent persona drift, and the result is floored at zero.

05KL-Divergence Constraint & Adaptive Beta

To prevent the RL engine from drifting the persona too far from the player's true behavioral profile, a KL-divergence penalty is computed at every step.

5.1 KL Divergence Computation

Given current persona distribution $\pi_t$ and frozen baseline $\pi_0$ (both normalized to the 16-dim simplex):

$$D_{\text{KL}}(\pi_t \| \pi_0) = \sum_{i=1}^{16} \pi_t^{(i)} \ln\!\frac{\pi_t^{(i)}}{\pi_0^{(i)}}$$

The penalty applied to the reward is $\beta \cdot D_{\text{KL}}$, where $\beta$ is the adaptive coefficient.

5.2 Adaptive Beta Scheduling

The coefficient $\beta$ adapts over time based on a rolling 5-step window of KL values:

avg_drift = mean(last 5 KL values)

if avg_drift > 0.3:   β ← min(β × 1.5, β_max)     # drift too high → increase penalty
if avg_drift < 0.05:  β ← max(β × 0.8, 0.01)       # drift negligible → relax penalty

Initialization: $\beta_0 = 0.1$, $\beta_{\max} = 1.0$

This creates a self-regulating feedback loop: if the PPO policy pushes traits too far from baseline, the penalty automatically increases, forcing the policy back. If traits stay close to the original persona, the penalty relaxes to allow beneficial adaptations.

06Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)

The PPO module applies a lightweight variant of the Proximal Policy Optimization algorithm directly on the persona logit vector — no neural network required.

6.1 Policy Representation

The policy is a softmax over logits:

$$\pi(\mathbf{z})_i = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_{j=1}^{16} e^{z_j}}, \quad \mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{16}$$

When initialized from a persona trait vector $\mathbf{t}$, the logits are computed as:

$$z_i = \ln\!\left(\frac{t_i}{\sum_j t_j} + \epsilon\right), \quad \epsilon = 10^{-8}$$

6.2 Clipped Surrogate Objective

Given the ratio of new to old probabilities:

$$r_i = \frac{\pi_{\text{new}}^{(i)}}{\pi_{\text{old}}^{(i)} + \epsilon}$$

The clipped surrogate loss is:

$$L^{\text{CLIP}} = -\frac{1}{16} \sum_{i=1}^{16} \min\!\left(r_i \cdot A_i,\; \text{clip}(r_i, 1{-}\varepsilon, 1{+}\varepsilon) \cdot A_i\right)$$

with clip range $\varepsilon = 0.2$ and advantages $A_i$ broadcast from the batch mean advantage.

6.3 Entropy Bonus

An entropy term prevents premature convergence to a single dominant trait:

$$H(\pi) = -\sum_{i=1}^{16} \pi_i \ln(\pi_i + \epsilon)$$

The entropy bonus coefficient is $c_e = 0.01$, ensuring trait diversity is maintained.

6.4 Gradient Update

The simplified policy gradient with entropy regularization:

$$\nabla_{\mathbf{z}} = -A \cdot (1 - \pi) - c_e \cdot (-\ln\pi - 1)$$ $$\mathbf{z}_{t+1} = \mathbf{z}_t - \alpha \cdot \nabla_{\mathbf{z}}$$

Learning rate $\alpha = 0.003$. Updates occur every 5 steps when sufficient transitions are buffered.

07Experience Replay & GAE Advantages

A circular buffer stores transitions for off-policy advantage estimation.

7.1 Transition Structure

Transition = {
    state:          {step: int}
    action:         {action_type, player_id, target_context, ...}
    reward:         float  (shaped reward after KL penalty)
    next_state:     {step: int}
    done:           bool
    persona_vector: float[16]
    advantage:      float  (computed by GAE)
}

7.2 Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE)

Advantages are computed in reverse over the buffer using TD(λ):

$$\delta_t = r_t + \gamma \cdot r_{t+1} \cdot (1 - d_t) - r_t$$ $$\hat{A}_t = \delta_t + \gamma \cdot \lambda \cdot (1 - d_t) \cdot \hat{A}_{t+1}$$

Parameters: $\gamma = 0.99$ (discount factor), $\lambda = 0.95$ (GAE smoothing). Buffer capacity: 500 transitions.

7.3 Batch Sampling

When the PPO update triggers (every 5 steps), a batch of up to 16 transitions is sampled uniformly from the buffer. The mean advantage from the batch is broadcast across all 16 trait dimensions to form the advantage vector for the PPO update.

08GraphRAG Knowledge Layer

The system uses an in-memory property graph (replacing Neo4j) with vector similarity search (replacing Zep Cloud) to provide structured context retrieval for agents.

8.1 Graph Ontology

Seven entity types are defined:

EntityKey PropertiesTypical Count per Episode
Playername, position, nationality, age1–11
Teamname, formation, style, league1–2
Leaguename, country, strength1
Matchhome/away teams, season, result5–10
Skillname, category (16 skill nodes per player)16
Injurytype, severity, games missed0–3

Fourteen relation types connect entities: PLAYS_FOR, HAS_SKILL, PARTICIPATED_IN, CHEMISTRY_WITH, HAS_MEMORY, and more.

8.2 Dual-Path Retrieval

The GraphRetriever combines two retrieval strategies:

Path 1: Structured Traversal (BFS)

A breadth-first search from the player node up to 2 hops, returning neighboring entities. This provides factual context: team, skills, past matches.

Path 2: Vector Similarity (Cosine)

$$\text{sim}(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{e}_i) = \frac{\mathbf{q} \cdot \mathbf{e}_i}{\|\mathbf{q}\| \cdot \|\mathbf{e}_i\|}$$

When a query embedding is provided, cosine similarity is computed against all stored node embeddings. The top-$K$ results ($K=3$) are returned.

8.3 Episodic Memory (Zep-style)

After each simulation round, a memory node is appended to the graph:

Player →[HAS_MEMORY]→ EpisodeMemory("Round 3: 2G 1A rating=8.1")

This provides agents with a growing narrative of the player's simulated performance, enabling the report agent to build coherent scouting narratives.

09Match Simulation Engine

The simulation engine generates structured match events that directly map to the player's persona traits. It is not a physics engine — it is a probabilistic event generator.

9.1 Event Generation

Each match round generates 5–25 events drawn from 12 types:

EVENT_TYPES = [pass, shot, goal, assist, dribble, tackle,
               intercept, foul, save, cross, header, key_pass]

Trait-Event Mapping

Each event type is influenced by 3 specific traits. For example:

EventPrimary Traits (indices)
GoalPositioning (2), Technical (3), Form Momentum (12)
DribbleSpeed (0), Technical (3), Creativity (7)
TackleWork Rate (8), Positioning (2), Pressure Tolerance (6)
Key PassCreativity (7), Decision Speed (5), Teamwork (15)

9.2 Event Sampling

Event type weights are computed from the normalized persona vector:

$$w_e = 1 + \sum_{j \in \text{traits}(e)} \pi_j, \quad \text{then}\; \hat{w}_e = \frac{w_e}{\sum_e w_e}$$

Event types are sampled from this categorical distribution using np.random.choice with weighted probabilities. Defensive events (tackle, intercept) receive a bonus proportional to opponent strength.

9.3 Success Probability

For each event, the success probability combines multiple factors:

$$P(\text{success}) = \text{clip}\!\left(0.3 + 0.5 \cdot S_{\text{skill}} - 0.2 \cdot O_{\text{opp}} + H - F + I,\; 0.05,\; 0.95\right)$$

where $S_{\text{skill}}$ is the sum of relevant trait values, $O_{\text{opp}}$ is opponent strength, $H$ is home advantage ($+0.05$ or $-0.02$), $F$ is fatigue penalty ($f \times 0.15$), and $I$ is match importance boost ($m \times 0.1$).

9.4 Rating Calculation

$$\text{Rating} = \text{clip}\!\left(6.0 + \sum_{e \in \text{events}} \Delta_e,\; 1.0,\; 10.0\right)$$

Each event contributes a rating impact $\Delta_e$ that depends on event type and success/failure. Goals add $+1.0$, assists $+0.6$, saves $+0.5$, while fouls deduct $-0.2$. Failed events receive $0.3\times$ the negative impact.

9.5 Fatigue Model

$$f_{t+1} = \min\!\left(1.0,\; f_t + 0.03 + 0.02 \cdot f_t\right)$$

Initial fatigue depends on position and stamina:

$$f_0 = f_{\text{position}} \times \max(0.01,\; 1 - \text{stamina})$$

Position baselines range from 0.02 (GK) to 0.09 (LW/RW), reflecting the physical demands of each role.

10Task Grading System

Three tasks of escalating difficulty are provided, each with a deterministic grader that produces a score in $[0, 1]$.

Task 1: Single Player Stat Prediction Easy

Binary classification: predict if key metric is above or below career mean.

$$G_1 = \min\!\left(1.0,\; 0.5 \cdot \mathbb{1}[\hat{y} = y] + 0.3 \cdot (1 - |c - \mathbb{1}[\hat{y} = y]|) + 0.2 \cdot Q_r\right)$$

where $c$ is the agent's confidence, $\mathbb{1}[\hat{y}=y]$ is the correctness indicator, and $Q_r$ is the reasoning quality (word count heuristic: <10 words→0.2, <50→0.5, 50+→0.8).

Max steps: 10

Task 2: Player-Team Tactical Fit Medium

Simulate 5 match rounds and grade each round on three sub-scores:

$$G_2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{r=1}^{N}\left(0.4 \cdot P_r + 0.4 \cdot T_r + 0.2 \cdot C_r\right)$$

where $P_r$ is output plausibility (goals ≤ 5 and rating in [4,10] → 0.8), $T_r$ is tactical alignment (ratio of successful events), and $C_r$ is narrative coherence (≥3 distinct event types → 0.8).

Max steps: 20

Task 3: Full Squad Recruitment Sim Hard

10-match season simulation for an 11-player squad, graded on 5 sub-scores:

$$G_3 = 0.30 \cdot S_{\text{output}} + 0.25 \cdot S_{\text{rotation}} + 0.20 \cdot S_{\text{injury}} + 0.15 \cdot S_{\text{tactical}} + 0.10 \cdot S_{\text{dev}}$$

Sub-graders:

  • Squad Output: $\text{clip}(\text{GPG}/2, 0, 1)$ — goals-per-game ratio
  • Rotation: $\text{clip}(\text{rotations}/(\text{matches} \times 0.5), 0, 1)$ — player rotation frequency
  • Injury Management: rest decisions vs. injuries ratio
  • Tactical Evolution: $\text{clip}(\text{formation changes}/3, 0, 1)$
  • Individual Development: player improvement ratio

Max steps: 50
